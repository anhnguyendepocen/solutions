Question 1

(a)

We wish to transform the \(X\) to \(Y\) such that \(Y = aX + b\). We are given that: \[ \begin{aligned} E[X] &= 35 \\ Var[X] &= 10^2 \end{aligned} \] and that we would like: \[ \begin{aligned} E[Y] &= 100 \\ Var[Y] &= 15^2 \end{aligned} \] Substituting \(Y = aX + b\) implies: \[ \begin{aligned} a E[X] + b &= 100 \\ a^2 Var[X] &= 15^2 \end{aligned} \] and substituing \(E[X] = 35\) and \(Var[X] = 10^2\) implies: \[ \begin{aligned} 35a + b = 100 &\implies b = 100 - 35a \\ 10^2 a^2 = 15^2 &\implies a^2 = \frac{15^2}{10^2} \end{aligned} \] so that \(a = 1.5\) and \(b = 47.5\).

(b)

An alternative transformation would be \(a = -1.5\) and \(b = 152\). However, this is problematic since it reorders the data \[ x_i > x_j \implies y_i < y_j \]

(b)

Note that the theoretical standard deviation is provided in the text file. However, it can be calculated as follows:

pr_dat <- mean(dat$proportion.girls)
sd_exp <- sqrt(pr_dat * (1 - pr_dat) / 3903)
print(sd_exp)
## [1] 0.008

The hint tells us that: \[ \frac{(n - 1) s ^ 2}{\sigma ^ 2} \sim \chi^2_{23} \] which we can compare to the lower and upper bounds of the 95% confidence interval for a \(\chi^2_{23}\):

tstat <- (23 * sd_dat ^ 2) / sd_exp ^ 2
lower <- qchisq(0.025, 23)
upper <- qchisq(0.975, 23)

# test
tstat < upper & tstat > lower
## [1] TRUE
